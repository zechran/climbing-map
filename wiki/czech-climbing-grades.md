# Czech Climbing Grades

**Summary**: The grading system used in Czech sandstone climbing, based on Roman numerals combined with letters, covering the full difficulty range from beginner to elite.

**Sources**: piskari.cz (climber comments as grade examples)

**Last updated**: 2026-05-25

---

Czech sandstone uses a distinct grading scale that combines **Roman numerals** with **lowercase letters** (a, b, c) to indicate difficulty. A star (*) can be added to indicate that a route is at the harder end of its grade or deserves special notice.

## Scale overview

| Grade | Approximate UIAA equiv. | Character |
|-------|------------------------|-----------|
| I – III | I – III | Scrambling / easy climbing |
| IV – V | IV – V | Moderate |
| VI | VI | Beginning of harder climbing |
| VIIa | VIIa (UIAA VI+/VII-) | Lower end of trad challenge |
| VIIb | UIAA VII | Mid |
| VIIc | UIAA VII+ | Upper end |
| VIIIa | UIAA VIII- | Demanding |
| VIIIb | UIAA VIII | Hard |
| VIIIc | UIAA VIII+ | Very hard |
| IXa–IXc | UIAA IX | Elite |
| Xa–Xc | UIAA X+ | World-class |

## Notes from route comments

- **Ženšen** is graded VIIa but climbers report it feeling like VIIc (source: piskari.cz comment 2026-05-18)
- **Chápeš, víš jak** is graded VIIIc; one climber argues it could be Xa (source: piskari.cz comment 2026-05-15)
- **Nenázvuhodná prasečina** is graded IXc via the natural line; the direct variant between 2nd and 3rd ring is considered Xa

The subjective nature of grades is a recurring theme in climber commentary — sandstone conditions (matrosh, wetness, vegetation) significantly affect perceived difficulty.

## Star ratings

A star (*) added to a grade (e.g. VIIa*) typically signals either that the route punches above its grade or is a must-do classic.
